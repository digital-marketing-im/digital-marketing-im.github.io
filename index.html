Welcome! During more than a decade working in digital marketing for a number of B2B investment management firms, I often came up against challenges and struggled to find any help that wasn’t purely theoretical. So, I decided to create something to help digital marketers succeed in what can be a very challenging industry.

Who is this guide for?

This guide is primarily aimed at digital marketing professionals who sit within the marketing teams of B2B investment management firms.

How will this guide help you?

This guide will help you to create your own digital marketing strategy. We use a fictional investment firm to demonstrate real examples for you to follow which you can adapt four your own projects.

It should also help you add structure to your strategy, introduce ideas for individual projects, enact change in how your team thinks, make your role a little more rewarding, and demonstrate the impact of your marketing activities to the wider business.

What is B2B investment management?

Business-to-consumer (B2C) refers to the process of selling products and services directly between a business and consumers who are the end-users of its products or services. As a business model, business-to-consumer differs significantly from the business-to-business (B2B) model, which refers to commerce between two or more businesses.

An example of a B2C company in financial services is Hargreaves Lansdown. As long as we meet certain criteria, we can open an investment account or an ISA. Within that accoutn we can buy or sell stocks, bonds and funds. The funds that you can invest in though HL will be managed by investment management firms. Some of those firms — such as Vanguard — will also allow you to directly invest in their funds if they have their own platform. Others, like PIMCO, will only work with other businesses rather than end investors.

If you work for a B2B investment management firm, you will most likely be targeting financial advisors and/or institutional investors, who may invest in your firm’s products on behalf of their clients – usually through an investment vehicle such as a fund or separately managed account (SMA). These investment products will contain stocks, bonds, or other instruments; portfolio managers will actively select these instruments based on their own views and research, or they may passively follow an index.

While they are invested, clients are charged a fee based on percentage of their portfolio value (detail about fees) – until they decide to sell. At any time, clients may top up, sell some or all of their investment, switch to a different product, invest in additional products, or rebalance their portfolio (which are often called flows).

Depending on your job, you will be required to focus on two main goals: (1) utilising a variety of marketing strategies and tactics to attract new prospects, and (2) servicing existing clients.
